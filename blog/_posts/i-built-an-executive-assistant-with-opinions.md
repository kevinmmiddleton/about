**[IMAGE: Hero. A tidy desk where a small assistant has laid out a calendar, a drafted email marked "draft," and three proposed time slots, while a person reviews and approves. Minimal, soft colors, white background, no text.]**

I built myself an executive assistant with opinions. It connects to my calendar, my email, and a database, and it acts like an opinionated EA who knows my preferences and protects my time without being asked.

### It knows my rules

It manages my calendar and meeting prep against rules I'd otherwise have to enforce by hand: interviews between 1 and 5, nothing before 10am, thirty-minute buffers so I'm not sprinting between calls, and weekends left alone. When a scheduling email comes in, it finds times that actually fit those rules and drafts a reply with three solid options.

### Drafts but never sends

**[IMAGE: A control panel with two big switches: "Draft" flipped on, "Send" firmly off; "Suggest" on, "Book" off. A human hand resting near them. Minimal, soft colors, no text.]**

This is the part I'm most deliberate about. It drafts but never sends. It suggests but never books. Every decision that touches my calendar or speaks for me still goes through me.

I wanted something that protects my time, not something that quietly runs my life. There's a version of this that just books things on your behalf, and it makes me nervous. The whole value of an assistant who knows your preferences disappears the moment it starts making commitments you didn't see. So it does all the work right up to the decision, and then it hands the decision to me.

### Showing up with context

**[IMAGE: A person walking into a meeting holding a single tidy briefing card about the other attendee, looking prepared and relaxed. Minimal, soft colors, no text.]**

Before a meeting with someone I don't know, it does the research I'd usually skip when I'm slammed: who they are, what we've emailed about before, what they're working on. For interviews, it pulls the role from my tracker so the context is right there. I show up with a point of view instead of winging it, which matters to me. Showing up to someone's time without having done the homework feels disrespectful, and it's exactly the kind of prep that falls off when you're busy.

### The loop I stole from Meg Bear

**[IMAGE: A cycle diagram: prep feeds the meeting, the meeting feeds notes, the notes feed the next prep. Minimal, soft colors, no text.]**

The newest piece I took straight from Meg Bear, who wrote about her own version of this on LinkedIn. After a meeting, the assistant writes the notes and action items back to my board, so the next conversation picks up where the last one ended. The prep feeds the synthesis, and the synthesis feeds the next prep. Each meeting makes the next one sharper, and I'm not relying on my own memory to carry context between them.

### On-demand and unattended

The same logic runs two ways. I can ask it things during the day ("what does my day look like," or forward it a scheduling email), and it also powers my automated morning briefing, the same assistant doing the same work while I'm still asleep.

It takes the logistics off my plate and leaves the judgment to me. That's the trade I actually want from AI: less wrangling, same control.
