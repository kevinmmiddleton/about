Can we automate how we handle rejection? I think so. And I mean that in both senses, because the way I built it took the sting out of the literal task and out of the feeling underneath it.

For months, logging rejections was a manual ritual. An email would land, I'd stop what I was doing, read the bad news, then update my tracker like I was filing a report on my own disappointment. The rejection itself wasn't the hard part. The ritual was. Every "no" came with a little tax: read it, process it, do data entry about it, then try to get back to your day.

### Casting a wide net, then narrowing it

**[IMAGE: A two-stage funnel. A wide net pulls in a mix of emails at the top; a second, finer filter lets only the real rejections through. Minimal, soft colors, no text.]**

Every morning, a scheduled task sweeps my inbox for the language of rejection. Around 30 phrases plus the usual applicant-tracking sender patterns cast a wide net. Then a second pass throws out the "thanks for applying" confirmations, so only the real rejections survive.

It runs in two stages on purpose. The cost of a false positive, marking a still-active application as dead, is much higher than the cost of a false negative, catching a rejection a day late. So I went wide first, then narrowed. Better to miss one until tomorrow than to quietly bury a live lead.

### Batching changed how it felt

**[IMAGE: A calendar week where twelve small storm clouds are gathered into one tidy morning summary card instead of scattered across the days. Minimal, soft colors, no text.]**

The first version handed me the batch to confirm. Even that small change mattered. Twelve rejections in one calm morning summary land completely differently than twelve separate gut-punches scattered across a week. Same information, a fraction of the emotional drag.

Then I moved my tracker to a real database, and the scanner could update it directly. Now I don't process rejections at all. I get a short note about what changed, like a weather report. Cloudy with a chance of "we decided to move forward with another candidate."

### The real win was emotional, not mechanical

**[IMAGE: A person closing a laptop and looking out a window with a coffee, unbothered, while a small assistant tidies up in the background. Minimal, soft colors, no text.]**

The time it saves is fine. What actually matters is that I don't have to personally absorb each one anymore. Five quiet minutes in the morning beats doing data entry on someone else's decision.

That's the design insight I keep coming back to. When you automate something, don't just hunt for the busywork. Look for the parts that quietly cost you something every time you touch them. The rejection scanner barely saves any time. It saves something more valuable than time.

You can't automate away rejection. But you can automate the part where you make it worse by handling it one painful email at a time.
